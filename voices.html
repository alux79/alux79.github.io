Voices from My Family

Interview

An informal interview with family members showed the important contrast between past experiences with education in Cambodia and modern approaches. This view gives a unique viewpoint, illustrating common perspectives that both parents and children had when interacting with Cambodian education, allowing for insight into more tensions between traditional values and modern reforms on a personal level.

Voices: Past Experiences

  • “If you showed up late, you’d have to stand outside the class.”
  • “Five wrong answers? Five hits on the palm. That was normal.”
  • “Back then, going to school was a privilege. Many families couldn’t afford it.”
  • “We weren’t allowed to ask why; if the teacher said it, you just did it.”
  • “You helped with the family business. School had to come second sometimes.”

Voices: Reflections Today

  • “Today, kids have more freedom, but sometimes they forget to respect.”
  • “Social media and travel opened student minds, but pulled them from traditions.”
  • “Authority should mean guidance, not blind obedience.”
  • “Mixing traditional values with modern education makes us stronger, not weaker.”
  • “Teachers need to be paid better. If we want better education, we must respect teachers first.”

Differences & Integration

These experiences show that Cambodian education was built on respect, responsibility, and a commitment to learning and family. Although many methods used to enforce these values, such as corporal punishment, are without question, considered harmful in today’s world, the core ideas driving those methods still provide important lessons. Respect for teachers, commitment to education, a sense of community, and many more ideas were not just encouraged, but expected.

However, as the Cambodian education system moves towards modern reforms, there is a very real risk of losing these foundations. New methods and freedoms do bring many benefits, but occasionally do so without careful thought. They may leave a void that discipline, respect and purpose had filled. Berkvens (2017) mentions that reforms that ignore cultural and traditional values often have struggled to fully succeed. We come back to the idea that the real question is how can we integrate the best parts of traditional values into modernized systems.
